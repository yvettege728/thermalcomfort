About this system

The Problem

Despite Gund Hall being a flawed building, we see governance and policy as the most immediate means to improve both sustainability and occupant agency.

→ ✗
Occupants
Discomfort is real, but it reaches power only as scattered complaints rather than a legible collective signal.
⇄ ✗
Facilities
Operations inherit a flawed building, but lack an interface that turns thermal feedback into ranked, actionable evidence.
⏸ ✗
Decision-Makers
Policy remains static, responsibility stays diffuse, and the institution keeps mistaking automation for governance.
Why the problem persists — the invisibility loop
POLICY
Static standards define comfort abstractly, before real occupants enter the picture.
→
EXECUTION
Automation runs the building, but no shared interface shows where policy and lived conditions diverge.
↑
↓
RESPONSE
Because signals never return as visible decisions, occupants learn that reporting discomfort changes nothing.
←
PERCEPTION
Discomfort is felt privately as noise, annoyance, or individual weakness rather than collective evidence.
Each stage obscures the next, so the loop sustains itself.
Our Position

Our design position is to make an interface and frame it as a tool for reconnecting feedback loops. The goal is not to pretend Gund is suddenly a good building; it is to make thermal evidence legible, shareable, and actionable enough that governance can improve both sustainability outcomes and people's agency within the building.

USER
Vote discomfort, see that it is shared, and understand that feedback enters a visible system.
OPERATOR
See aggregated patterns, rank them spatially, and record what action was taken in response.
DECISION
Reconnect policy to lived evidence, so governance decisions can be justified against visible feedback.

03The model architecture

This section shows how we got from the problem to the design — the reasoning arc from diagnosis to prototype. Every element of this dashboard belongs to one step in this chain.

1 · Diagnosis — what is actually broken
The HVAC operates within policy. The problem is a governance and information failure: discomfort signals are suppressed by friction before they reach anyone with authority to act; facilities lack a choice architecture that makes the right action also the easiest action; decision-makers have delegated the problem to automation while the university temperature policy has been static since 2009.
Goetz 2011 (Wired) · Tett 2015 (Silo Effect) · Ostrom 1990 (commons governance) · Harvard E&U Temperature Policy 2009
2 · Causal claim — why the intervention works at the information level
If the problem is that discomfort fails to reach decision-makers at the right time in the right form, the fix does not necessarily require renewing the building, as in Ben's envelope-first instinct. What is broken is the information architecture: discomfort is felt but never aggregated; aggregated complaints exist but never reach the person with authority; the person with authority has no frame that makes thermal governance feel like their job. The building stays the same. What changes is the institutional capacity to act on what the building is already telling you. This is why the intervention is a new thermal interface for communication, not a construction project. The goal: a round-trip path for discomfort — sensing → decision → action → re-sensing — completed through nudging that makes the right information easily available at the moment a decision is being made.
Meadows (2008) leverage points · Schön & Rein (1994) frame reflection · Steinberg two-governments framework
3 · Four functional phases — what the intervention actually does
P1Metric Collection Temperature sensors + occupant survey establish the baseline. Evidence exists for the first time in a form that can be aggregated and cited.
P2Deploy Feedback QR codes, the app, and the public dashboard go live for students, visitors, and staff. Friction-to-voice drops from ~5 minutes to ~10 seconds. Plurality becomes visible: occupants learn they are not alone.
P3Evaluate Feedback Aggregated signals are reviewed against the metric baseline. Do the patterns hold? Where are the cold clusters? The dashboard gives facilities and leadership a shared object for interpretation.
P4Implement Change Change can mean adjusting a setpoint, restructuring who has decision authority, or removing a player who blocks the loop. The type of change is determined by what the feedback reveals — not predetermined.
Phases are cumulative, not sequential replacements. Each phase adds one functional capacity while keeping prior phases running.
4 · Ripple effect
When thermal comfort is addressed through institutional mechanisms that remediate resources and create a shared grammar of thermal comfort, other effects begin to emerge naturally. What we first mapped as separate scenarios can become linked consequences of the same redesigned feedback loop: setpoint adjustment, governance restructuring, and policy revision all become more thinkable once discomfort is made legible and actionable.

OPERATORFacilities A spatial heatmap of where discomfort is clustering, not a queue of individual complaints. A one-click action log that records what was done and when. The choice architecture removes "I don't know what to do" as a reason not to act.

Read top → down: each step is built on the one before. The interface is not the product — it is the demonstration that a repaired feedback loop is possible within existing organizational constraints.

04Nudging mechanisms

Every interface element in this system is built around a behavioral nudge. The nudges are not decorative — they are the mechanism by which the information architecture produces different behavior.

Mechanism
Loss aversion
+ Anchoring
Thermal discomfort reframed as value already paid for and now lost. $51.14/hr tuition anchor converts an invisible complaint into a concrete institutional cost. Losses feel roughly twice as motivating as equivalent gains (Kahneman & Tversky 1979), so framing inaction as destruction of value rather than absence of comfort changes the emotional stakes of doing nothing.
Reaches
Users
Every occupant understands wasted money. The $358/week vote consequence card makes the personal cost visible.
Operators
$430k/month at building scale turns a comfort complaint into a budget conversation.
Decision-makers
$5.1M/year institutional loss is a strategic line item, not a facilities anecdote.
Where in the system
R5 consequence card across all three modes. Dollar anchor is the same number at three time horizons: weekly, monthly, annual.
Mechanism
Information provision
+ Choice architecture
Better-organized information does not change what people want. It changes whether the right action is also the easiest action (Thaler & Sunstein 2008). The current system gives facilities no spatial map, no threshold for what counts as actionable, no record of prior responses. This system restructures the decision environment so the best choice is also the most accessible one.
Reaches
Operators
Aggregated heatmap + threshold alerts replace an inbox of unranked complaints. One-click action log removes "I don't know what to do" as a reason not to act.
Decision-makers
Coalition scores and productivity dimensions translate comfort data into the language of resource allocation.
Where in the system
Operator spatial heatmap. Action log. Decision mode scenario sliders and coalition heatmap.
Mechanism
Cognitive load reduction
Decision-makers receive thermal complaints as isolated, ad-hoc events with no common unit of measure. Reducing cognitive load does not simplify the decision — it removes the noise that currently stops the signal from reaching the person with authority. The interface does the work of translation so the decision-maker can focus on the decision itself.
Reaches
Decision-makers
Five outcome metrics, one dollar anchor, one coalition score. The Dean can see a $5.1M/year cost without constructing that frame themselves.
Where in the system
Decision mode executive summary. ROI ratio. M48 end-state projection.
Loss aversion creates the motivational pressure that makes people want to act. Choice architecture ensures that acting is easier than not acting. Cognitive load reduction removes the translation burden that currently prevents the signal from reaching the person with authority to respond. Together they produce a system where action is accessible at every level of the organization.

04bThe information effect

DECISION mode §04 makes a simple behavioral claim: once you have seen the evidence that this system produces, you will make a different institutional decision than you made before. The interaction tests this directly.

In §01, you pick a persona and make decisions with no aggregate data — only narrative context, exactly as decision-makers currently operate. In §04, the same trigger moment is replayed, but first you receive five structured facts: the isolation index, the friction baseline, the loop closure time, the dollar cost, and a named stakeholder quote. Then you choose again.

A shift toward convening and deliberation is the evidence that the nudge worked. The information did not change what you want — it changed what the situation looked like. The 70% isolation figure is typically the hinge: when decision-makers realize that what appeared to be scattered individual complaints is actually a building-wide pattern hiding behind an information failure, the frame shifts from "managing a complaint" to "addressing a governance gap." That reframe is the entire intervention. The dashboard doesn't add new leverage to the institution — it makes the existing leverage legible.

No shift is also informative. It indicates either that you already chose to convene (the nudge succeeded at baseline) or that the information alone is insufficient — which would mean the institutional incentives around the decision-maker have not changed, and a choice architecture intervention would need to go deeper than information provision. That is the boundary condition for where S02 stops and S03 (governance restructuring) begins.

05Behind the numbers

Every projected number in this system is computed, not asserted. Below are the four core formulas that drive the model. Every dollar figure, metric prediction, and political coalition score on this dashboard can be traced back to one of these four equations.

5.1 · Tuition-hour anchor (R5 across all modes)

The cross-mode dollar value is built on a single ratio: how much tuition is "spent" per hour of studio time at GSD. This anchor lets us translate hours of thermal discomfort into dollars users already understand.

Tuition per hour $/hour=$90,000÷10 months÷22 days÷8 hours=$51.14 $90k = approximate GSD annual tuition. 10 / 22 / 8 = standard academic year, working days, studio hours. Each hour of thermal discomfort is therefore one hour of paid-for-but-unusable time.
Recovered tuition value value=hours_recovered×$51.14 USER (per voter, weekly): 7 h × $51.14 ≈ $358. OPERATOR (whole building, monthly): 8,400 h × $51.14 ≈ $430k. DECISION (whole building, annual): 100,000 h × $51.14 ≈ $5.1M. Same anchor, three time windows. The 7 h/week comes from the P2 survey median (~4–10 h/wk reported as "wasted to layering up / leaving the building"). 8,400 h/mo ≈ 500 occupants × 7 h/wk × 4.3 wk/mo × ~56% effective coverage. 100,000 h/yr is the projected steady-state once the feedback loop is closed (S02 scenario; S03 full deployment projects 130,000 h).
5.2 · What If metric prediction (DECISION mode)

When you drag the P1 / P2 / P3 sliders in DECISION mode, each metric prediction is computed by combining the per-phase effect of each intervention level. Each metric has a baseline and a 3-phase effect matrix giving low and high deltas.

Metric projection Δ_low = Σᵢ ( e[m].pᵢ.low × sliderᵢ/100 )
Δ_high = Σᵢ ( e[m].pᵢ.high × sliderᵢ/100 )
predicted_range = [ baseline + Δ_low , baseline + Δ_high ] For each metric m and each phase i ∈ {P1, P2, P3}, multiply the phase's low/high effect by the slider position (as a fraction). Sum across phases to get the total delta range.

The effect matrix (defined in shared.js) holds 5 metrics × 3 phases × 2 bounds = 30 numbers. Each is grounded in the literature listed in References at the bottom of this page or in our own GSD survey (n = 24 to date).

5.3 · Coalition score (DECISION mode heatmap)

The 8-stakeholder × 3-scenario heatmap quantifies political viability. Each cell is a single judgement call: how does this scenario affect this stakeholder, on a −3 (strong opposition) to +3 (strong support) scale. The judgement is informed by P2 stakeholder interviews and is intentionally simple — a coalition column is the column-sum across all 8 stakeholders.

Stakeholder score (scoring rule) score[s, scenario] ∈ { −3, −2, −1, 0, +1, +2, +3 } −3 = veto-strength opposition. 0 = neutral or split. +3 = active champion. Each cell is grounded in stated positions: e.g. Facilities & Campus Ops scores +3 on S01 (status quo, no new work) and −2 on S03 (governance restructuring requires their cooperation in ways the current org chart does not authorize).
Coalition total per scenario coalition[scenario] = Σ_s ( score[s, scenario] ) Sum down each column. Current totals from shared.js: S01 = −4 (status quo loses on net — students/staff/faculty all oppose, only Facilities supports), S02 = +15 (recommended — broad support, only Facilities is neutral, no veto), S03 = +7 (full deployment — still positive but Facilities flips to −2). The S02 win is not the highest ceiling but the absence of veto-strength opposition. That is what makes it implementable.
5.4 · ROI ratio (DECISION mode R5)

The ROI shown in DECISION mode is the simplest formula in the model — but the choice of numerator is the design ethic. Comfort hours, not steam savings, is what we elect to put in the numerator.

Annual ROI ROI = tuition_value_recovered ÷ implementation_cost
= 100,000 h × $51.14 ÷ $80,000 ≈ 63 : 1 GSD spends $454k/year on steam regardless of any intervention (Harvard Capital Planning data). Energy savings from this proposal are small relative to that fixed cost — the comfort gain dwarfs them by ~150×. If we put steam savings (~$3k/yr modeled co-benefit) in the numerator instead, ROI would be ~0.04 : 1 and the project would not pencil out. The choice of numerator is the design ethic: recovered tuition hours (the comfort frame) rather than energy savings (the engineering frame).

All numbers across the three modes can be traced back to one of these four formulas. No magic, no hidden multipliers. If a number on the dashboard surprises you, you can recompute it from the equation above.

06References & sources

The model draws from two literature traditions. Behavioral mechanism research grounds how users interact with the dashboard. Thermal comfort, building performance, and energy literature grounds the metric definitions and baseline values.

A · Behavioral mechanisms

This group explains why visibility, framing, and reduced friction can change whether discomfort becomes a signal that people actually act on.

▸ Show detailed citations
Prentice, D. A. & Miller, D. T. (1993). "Pluralistic ignorance and alcohol use on campus: Some consequences of misperceiving the social norm." Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 64(2), 243–256.
→ Used in M02 Isolation Index. Justifies the ~70% baseline (people overestimate how alone they are in feeling discomfort) and the projected drop to ~25% after the aggregate readout intervention.
Thaler, R. H. & Sunstein, C. R. (2008). Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness. Yale University Press.
→ Used in M03 Friction-to-Voice. Justifies the design choice of 5-tier one-tap voting (vs. an email-form ticket): friction below 30 seconds substantially shifts participation rates.
Cialdini, R. B., Reno, R. R. & Kallgren, C. A. (1990). "A focus theory of normative conduct: Recycling the concept of norms to reduce littering in public places." Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 58(6), 1015–1026.
→ Used in M04 Loop Closure Time. The descriptive/prescriptive distinction motivates public response logs: facilities don't just respond, they respond visibly, which sets a norm.
Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A. (1979). "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk." Econometrica, 47(2), 263–291.
→ Used in R5 Consequence card across all 3 modes. Loss aversion (loss feels ~2× a gain) explains why "you lost ~$358 of comfort hours this week" works better than "you gained $3 of energy savings".
Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1974). "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." Science, 185(4157), 1124–1131.
→ Used in tuition anchor ($51 / hour). Anchoring effect: numbers are evaluated relative to a reference. We chose tuition because every GSD user has it as a salient sunk cost.
Thaler, R. H. (1985). "Mental Accounting and Consumer Choice." Marketing Science, 4(3), 199–214.
→ Used in tuition anchor design. Mental accounting explains why tuition feels distinct from other money — placing thermal hours in the tuition account makes them feel relevant in a way generic dollar values would not.
Goetz, T. (2011). "Harnessing the Power of Feedback Loops." Wired Magazine, June 2011.
→ Frames the entire diagnosis. The four-step feedback loop (Evidence → Relevance → Consequence → Action) is exactly what's broken at Gund — and what each region of the v6 dashboard restores, one step per region.

B · Thermal comfort, building performance, and energy

This group grounds the baseline comfort envelope, the meaning of the sensor gap, and the broader institutional framing around building performance.

▸ Show detailed citations
de Dear, R. & Brager, G. S. (2002). "Thermal comfort in naturally ventilated buildings: revisions to ASHRAE Standard 55." Energy and Buildings, 34(6), 549–561.
→ Used in M01 Perception Gap interpretation. Adaptive comfort theory: when occupants gain perceived control, the felt-vs-ambient gap narrows by 60–85%. This bounds our M01 projection range.
ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 55 (2020). Thermal Environmental Conditions for Human Occupancy. ASHRAE.
→ Defines the thermal comfort envelope used by Harvard FAS HVAC. Our M01 baseline (−4.1°F gap) is measured against this standard's PMV-PPD model.
Shove, E. (2003). Comfort, Cleanliness and Convenience: The Social Organization of Normality. Berg.
→ Justifies why private clothing/adaptation behavior is a useful diagnostic of broken feedback. Practices theory: when system response fails, people compensate privately — and that compensation is invisible to facilities.
Tett, G. (2015). The Silo Effect: The Peril of Expertise and the Promise of Breaking Down Barriers. Simon & Schuster.
→ Diagnostic framing for the broken feedback loop. The information silos between Mike (BAS), Ben (Facilities), Trevor (Sustainability), and students are exactly the silos this dashboard tries to bridge.
Ostrom, E. (1990). Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action. Cambridge University Press.
→ Names the project: Gund Thermal Commons. Thermal comfort is treated here as a shared resource requiring collective governance, not a private optimization problem.
Hussey, M. A. & Hughes, J. P. (2007). "Design and analysis of stepped wedge cluster randomized trials." Contemporary Clinical Trials, 28(2), 182–191.
→ Methodology for piloting S02. Stepped-wedge design lets us roll out the dashboard tray-by-tray rather than all-at-once, generating evidence while still serving every zone within the trial period.

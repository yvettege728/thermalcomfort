About this system

Gund Thermal Commons is a feedback-driven dashboard for thermal governance at Harvard GSD. One platform, three role-specific views, one shared data layer.

01What is this?

Gund Hall has a thermal problem. Not because the HVAC is broken — it works fine. The problem is that occupants can't signal discomfort cheaply, facilities can't see aggregated discomfort, and decision-makers don't see thermal as a tuition-value issue. The feedback loop is broken at every layer.

This system makes the invisible legible. It uses three role-specific surfaces — USER, OPERATOR, and DECISION — to give each person what they need to act, while keeping all three views connected to the same underlying data.

03Theory of change

The proposal is not a switch — it is a 48-month transition. Three phases of organizational change, ten actors, each moving at a different pace. Phases are cumulative interventions, not separate experiments: Phase 1 deploys Sensing; Phase 2 keeps Sensing running and adds Voicing; Phase 3 keeps both and adds Governing. 06Behind the numbers

Every projected number in this system is computed, not asserted. But the model has two kinds of computation, and they are not the same.

Quantitative formulas
Mechanical: take fixed inputs and produce numbers via algebra.
Example: M04 baseline = 18 days. Phase 2 effect = −9 to −13 days. If slider = 100%, result = 18 − 11.5 = 6.5 days. Reproducible.
Judgment rules
Design choices: encode a weighting or threshold that requires defending as a position.
Example: We chose tuition value as ROI numerator, not steam savings. This reframes comfort from "operations expense" to "tuition fulfillment." Contestable.

In defense, formulas get verified; judgment rules get contested. Below are the four core formulas and the three judgment rules they embody.

6.1 · Tuition-hour anchor (R5 across all modes)

The cross-mode dollar value is built on a single ratio: how much tuition is "spent" per hour of studio time at GSD. This anchor lets us translate hours of thermal discomfort into dollars users already understand.

Tuition per hour $/hour=$90,000÷10 months÷22 days÷8 hours=$51.14 $90k = approximate GSD annual tuition. 10 / 22 / 8 = standard academic year, working days, studio hours. Each hour of thermal discomfort is therefore one hour of paid-for-but-unusable time.
Recovered tuition value value=hours_recovered×$51.14 Example: 47 h × $51.14 ≈ $2,400. Same formula applies in OPERATOR (monthly hours) and DECISION (annual hours), producing weekly / monthly / annual figures from the same ratio.
6.2 · What If metric prediction (DECISION mode)

When you drag the P1 / P2 / P3 sliders in DECISION mode, each metric prediction is computed by combining the per-phase effect of each intervention level. Each metric has a baseline and a 3-phase effect matrix giving low and high deltas.

Metric projection Δ_low = Σᵢ ( e[m].pᵢ.low × sliderᵢ/100 )
Δ_high = Σᵢ ( e[m].pᵢ.high × sliderᵢ/100 )
predicted_range = [ baseline + Δ_low , baseline + Δ_high ] For each metric m and each phase i ∈ {P1, P2, P3}, multiply the phase's low/high effect by the slider position (as a fraction). Sum across phases to get the total delta range.

The effect matrix (defined in shared.js) holds 5 metrics × 3 phases × 2 bounds = 30 numbers. Each is grounded in the literature cited in §06 below or in our own GSD survey (n = 24 to date).

6.3 · Coalition score (DECISION mode heatmap)

The 8-stakeholder × 3-scenario heatmap quantifies political viability. Each cell is a weighted sum of how a scenario affects six concern dimensions, weighted by how much that stakeholder cares about each dimension.

Stakeholder payoff under scenario payoff[s, scenario] = Σ_d ( impact[scenario, d] × care[s, d] ) For each of the 6 dimensions d ∈ {Fixed Cost, Variable Cost, Comfort, Productivity, Coordination, Efficiency}, multiply the scenario's impact (−2 to +2) by the stakeholder's care weight (0 = low, 0.5 = mid, 1 = high). Sum across all 6 dimensions.
Coalition total coalition[scenario] = Σ_s ( payoff[s, scenario] ) Sum across all 8 stakeholders. Higher = stronger political coalition. Current totals: S02 = +12, S03 = +7, S01 = −4. The S02 win comes not from highest ceiling but from absence of veto-strength opposition (no cell darker than 0).
6.4 · ROI ratio (S02 vs S03 comparison)

The ROI shown in DECISION mode R5 is where the two recommended scenarios are directly compared. The choice of numerator is the design ethic: comfort hours, not steam savings, is what we elect to put in the numerator.

Scenario 2 (Sensing + Voicing, RECOMMENDED) ROI(S02) = 100,000 h × $51.14 ÷ $80,000 ≈ 64 : 1 Sensing (public temperature stations) + Voicing (QR vote + dashboard). 48-month deployment, months 0–24.
Scenario 3 (Sensing + Voicing + Governing, FULL SYSTEM) ROI(S03) = 130,000 h × $51.14 ÷ $140,000 ≈ 47 : 1 Adds Governing (Thermal Council + RCT protocol). Full 48-month deployment, months 0–48. Higher absolute comfort hours, but higher cost and lower coalition strength.

The strategic insight: S03 produces 30,000 more annual comfort hours than S02, but costs $60k more to implement, reducing ROI from 64:1 to 47:1. More importantly, S03's political coalition score drops from +12 to +7 — Facilities moves from neutral (0) to opposition (−2) because Thermal Council governance redistributes decision-making power away from their domain. S02 is recommended because it maximizes both financial efficiency AND political durability.

If we used steam savings (~$3k/year) as the numerator instead of tuition value, S02's ROI would be ~0.04 : 1 — and the project would not pencil out. The argument is comfort, with energy as a side benefit.

6.6 · What the model claims (and does not claim)
What we claim
  • The intervention will move five metrics in a specified direction and order (M02 / M03 first, M04 second, M01 third)
  • S02's coalition is broader than S01 or S03 (no veto-strength opposition)
  • Recovered tuition value at S02: ~$5M/year at 64:1 ROI
  • Phases are cumulative and necessary
What we do NOT claim
  • Exact effect sizes (all deltas are ranges, not points)
  • That comfort is directly measured (M01–M05 are feedback metrics)
  • That coalition scores are surveyed (they are inferred)
  • That the model is predictive in a frequentist sense (it is structured projection)

07References

The model draws from two literature traditions. Behavioral mechanism research grounds how users interact with the dashboard. Thermal comfort, building performance, and energy literature grounds the metric definitions and baseline values. Each reference is paired with a one-line note showing how it became part of the v6 model.

A · Behavioral mechanisms

Prentice, D. A. & Miller, D. T. (1993). "Pluralistic ignorance and alcohol use on campus: Some consequences of misperceiving the social norm." Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 64(2), 243–256.
→ Used in M02 Isolation Index. Justifies the ~70% baseline (people overestimate how alone they are in feeling discomfort) and the projected drop to ~25% after the aggregate readout intervention.
Thaler, R. H. & Sunstein, C. R. (2008). Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness. Yale University Press.
→ Used in M03 Friction-to-Voice. Justifies the design choice of 5-tier one-tap voting (vs. an email-form ticket): friction below 30 seconds substantially shifts participation rates.
Cialdini, R. B., Reno, R. R. & Kallgren, C. A. (1990). "A focus theory of normative conduct: Recycling the concept of norms to reduce littering in public places." Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 58(6), 1015–1026.
→ Used in M04 Loop Closure Time. The descriptive/prescriptive distinction motivates public response logs: facilities don't just respond, they respond visibly, which sets a norm.
Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A. (1979). "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk." Econometrica, 47(2), 263–291.
→ Used in R5 Consequence card across all 3 modes. Loss aversion (loss feels ~2× a gain) explains why "you lost $2,400 of comfort hours" works better than "you gained $3 of energy savings".
Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1974). "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." Science, 185(4157), 1124–1131.
→ Used in tuition anchor ($51 / hour). Anchoring effect: numbers are evaluated relative to a reference. We chose tuition because every GSD user has it as a salient sunk cost.
Thaler, R. H. (1985). "Mental Accounting and Consumer Choice." Marketing Science, 4(3), 199–214.
→ Used in tuition anchor design. Mental accounting explains why tuition feels distinct from other money — placing thermal hours in the tuition account makes them feel relevant in a way generic dollar values would not.
Goetz, T. (2011). "Harnessing the Power of Feedback Loops." Wired Magazine, June 2011.
→ Frames the entire diagnosis. The four-step feedback loop (Evidence → Relevance → Consequence → Action) is exactly what's broken at Gund — and what each region of the v6 dashboard restores, one step per region.

B · Thermal comfort, building performance, and energy

de Dear, R. & Brager, G. S. (2002). "Thermal comfort in naturally ventilated buildings: revisions to ASHRAE Standard 55." Energy and Buildings, 34(6), 549–561.
→ Used in M01 Perception Gap interpretation. Adaptive comfort theory: when occupants gain perceived control, the felt-vs-ambient gap narrows by 60–85%. This bounds our M01 projection range.
ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 55 (2020). Thermal Environmental Conditions for Human Occupancy. ASHRAE.
→ Defines the thermal comfort envelope used by Harvard FAS HVAC. Our M01 baseline (−4.1°F gap) is measured against this standard's PMV-PPD model.
Shove, E. (2003). Comfort, Cleanliness and Convenience: The Social Organization of Normality. Berg.
→ Justifies why private clothing/adaptation behavior is a useful diagnostic of broken feedback. Practices theory: when system response fails, people compensate privately — and that compensation is invisible to facilities.
Tett, G. (2015). The Silo Effect: The Peril of Expertise and the Promise of Breaking Down Barriers. Simon & Schuster.
→ Diagnostic framing for the broken feedback loop. The information silos between Mike (BAS), Ben (Facilities), Trevor (Sustainability), and students are exactly the silos this dashboard tries to bridge.
Ostrom, E. (1990). Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action. Cambridge University Press.
→ Names the project: Gund Thermal Commons. Thermal comfort is treated here as a shared resource requiring collective governance, not a private optimization problem.
Hussey, M. A. & Hughes, J. P. (2007). "Design and analysis of stepped wedge cluster randomized trials." Contemporary Clinical Trials, 28(2), 182–191.
→ Methodology for piloting S02. Stepped-wedge design lets us roll out the dashboard tray-by-tray rather than all-at-once, generating evidence while still serving every zone within the trial period.

Each metric prediction is a weighted blend of these literature sources, our own GSD survey data, and stakeholder reaction interviews (Ben Szalewicz, Mike Smith, Trevor O'Brien). The full assumptions document — with discount factors, boundary conditions, and what would still need confirming — is available on request.
